Introduction and Motivation

The study of gene expression patterns in the neocortex is a vital and very interesting area of neuroscience research because it has the potential to reveal how different neuronal subpopulations are organized and function within the human brain, thereby contributing to our overall understanding of the brain.

This dataset comes from a study about  single-nucleus transcriptomes from the human primary motor cortex (M1) and contains 76,533 nuclei from two post-mortem human samples. This project utilizes the files that contain the  trimmed means by cluster and metadata.  The high-resolution transcriptomic data obtained in this study is going to allows us to identify distinct neuronal subpopulations based on their gene expression profiles thereby offering insights into the cellular diversity within the M1 cortex.

The motivation for examining the M1 cortex lies in its critical role in voluntary movement and motor control, as well as its involvement in disorders such as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) and Parkinson’s disease.  More than that, studying the molecular diversity in this region of the brain  aligns with my desire to understand the molecular basis of brain function.

Literature Context

Previous research has shown that neuronal populations in the neocortex can be differentiated based on their gene expression profiles and laminar distribution. Watakabe et al. (2007) examined layer-specific gene expression in the mammalian neocortex where it compared between monkeys and mice. This study identified genes like ER81, Nurr1, CTGF, and 5-HT2C, which it found to exhibit both conserved layer preferences and significant interspecies differences. Essentially, these findings highlighted how specific neuronal subpopulations can be categorized based on gene expression, offering insights into understanding evolutionary adaptations.

Miller et al. (2019), on the other hand, discussed how the cellular diversity of the human cerebral cortex emerges through development and is organized in a way that supports complex cognitive functions. Their findings support the idea that cortical organization can vary greatly among species, while maintaining fundamental features that are conserved.

Vawter et al. (2004) focused on gender-specific gene expression in post-mortem human brains, highlighting how differential expression of X- and Y-linked genes can be detected using microarray technology. They found that certain genes linked to sex chromosomes show distinct patterns of expression between males and females, suggesting that sex-specific gene expression might play a role in neural function and potential vulnerability to neuropsychiatric disorders.

Jacques et al. (2011) investigated dynamic neural networks that supported memory retrieval and found that specific cortical regions do indeed show unique gene expression profiles that correspond with their functions. This study lends great support to my  approach to examining the M1 cortex, with aims to map the molecular architecture that correlates with motor function and its distinct characteristics compared to other cortical regions.

As such, these are the four hypothesis, my project focuses on

  1. Gene expression profiles differ significantly between SST and VIP neuron subclasses.

  2. Donor sex may result in small but noticeable differences in gene expression within some neuron types.

  3. Genes involved in neurotransmission will vary across different cortical layers.

  4. Specific marker genes are strongly associated with GABAergic, Glutamatergic, or Non-neuronal cell classes.

By using the combined insights from these earlier studies and the high-resolution transcriptomic data, I aim to understand more about the M1 regions by seeing if the data analysis done in this project can provide some insight into the chose hypothesis there furthering my knowledge of the M1 cortex.

Methods and Data Wrangling.

The statistical analysis followed a structured approach in order to ensure consistency and accuracy across all hypotheses. It began by importing the two data sets by them read into R studio followed by an examinations of these datasets using glimpse() and summary() functions to get an overview of the data structure.

For each hypothesis, the relevant data subset was extracted. For example in H1, SST and VIP neurons were extracted, for H2, I looked specifically at the male and female samples. For H3, the focus was on neurotransmission-related genes across various cortical layers, and for H4, I only looked at gene expression data by GABAergic, Glutamatergic, and Non-neuronal cell classes.

 After the relevant columns were selected, the data was cleaned. I calculated the proportion of zeros per gene, then filtered out genes with more than 95% zero values. This was  all done to reduce noise so that the statistical analysis was focused only on genes that provided meaningful expression levels.

Once the data was cleaned, depending on the number of groups that we were comparing, the relevant statistical tests for each hypothesis was performed. Only two types of tests have been used within this project. One of them being the Two-Sample T-Tests for comparing two groups (like male vs. female) and ANOVA for comparing multiple groups (like different cortical layers or cell classes). After obtaining p-values,  only the results that showed a significance (p < 0.05) were kept.

Finally, I also visualized the significant results using various plots. For H1 and H2,  double bar plots were used  to compare expression levels between groups. For H3, a boxplot was used to visualize gene expression across cortical layers. And finally For H4, a boxplot as well as a density plot to show gene expression variability among cell classes.

# t-test (Had to modify the t test code from class using sourcegraph because that t-test was coming out a little wonky) 
t_test_results <- lapply(1:nrow(sst_gene_expression_filtered), function(i) {
  
  sst_values <- as.numeric(sst_gene_expression_filtered[i, -1])
  vip_values <- as.numeric(vip_gene_expression_filtered[i, -1])
  

  if (length(sst_values) > 1 && length(vip_values) > 1) {
    t_res <- tryCatch(
      t.test(sst_values, vip_values),
      error = function(e) NULL
    )
    
    if (!is.null(t_res)) {
      return(data.frame(
        Gene = sst_gene_expression_filtered$feature[i],
        t_value = round(t_res$statistic, 2), 
        df = round(t_res$parameter, 2), 
        p_value = ifelse(t_res$p.value < 0.001, "< .001", round(t_res$p.value, 3)), 
        conf_int_low = round(t_res$conf.int[1], 2), 
        conf_int_high = round(t_res$conf.int[2], 2), 
        mean_diff = round(t_res$estimate[1] - t_res$estimate[2], 2)
      ))
    }
  }
  return(NULL)
})

  # NA values
  valid <- !is.na(expression_values)
  expression_values <- expression_values[valid]
  group_labels <- group_labels[valid]
  
 
  anova_result <- aov(expression_values ~ group_labels)
  
  
  p_value <- summary(anova_result)[[1]][["Pr(>F)"]][1]
  

  anova_results[[i]] <- data.frame(
    Gene = gene,
    p_value = p_value
  )
}

Discussion

The analysis of the data set with respect to the four hypotheses this project focused on  revealed distinct patterns of gene expression related to neuron subtypes, donor sex, cortical layers, and major cell classes.

For Neuron Subtypes (SST vs. VIP): I found significant differences in gene expression between SST and VIP neurons, particularly when it comes to  genes like HSDL2 and RIPPLY2. This therefore points out that specific genes may indeed play unique roles in these neuron subtypes.

Donor Sex Differences: The analysis her revealed several genes, including MAGI2 and TAB1, with do show  sex-related differences. These findings indicate that gene expression might be influenced by biological sex, which could have big implications for when it comes to understanding sex-specific neural functions.

Cortical Layer Variability: Genes involved in neurotransmission, like GRIN1 and SLC17A7, did show variability across different cortical layers, highlighting some potential functional differences in signal transmission depending on the cortical region.

Marker Genes and Cell Classes: We identified specific marker genes, such as NUP35 and STPG1, that showed significantly different expression levels among GABAergic, Glutamatergic, and Non-neuronal cell classes, throwing some support behind  the idea that gene expression is closely linked to cell type.

Overall, all four hypotheses within this project had their null hypothesis rejected, indicating that their alternate counterparts (aka the hypothesis themselves) might have some kernal of truth to them and that those need to be further researched and analyzed to be more definitive.
